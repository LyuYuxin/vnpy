## What is Quantitative Trading?

**Quantitative trading** (or "quant trading") is a systematic approach to trading financial instruments using mathematical models, statistical analysis, and computer algorithms. Instead of making trading decisions based on intuition or emotion, quantitative traders rely on:

- **Data-driven analysis**: Using historical price data, volume, and other market indicators
- **Mathematical models**: Statistical and machine learning models to predict market movements
- **Automated execution**: Computer programs that automatically execute trades based on predefined rules

Think of it as bringing the power of data science and automation to financial markets.

## Why Use Quantitative Trading?

### Advantages

1. **Emotion-Free Trading**: Removes psychological biases and emotional decision-making
2. **Backtesting**: Test your strategies on historical data before risking real money
3. **Speed and Efficiency**: Computers can analyze data and execute trades much faster than humans
4. **Consistency**: Strategies are applied systematically without deviation
5. **24/7 Monitoring**: Automated systems can monitor markets around the clock
6. **Scalability**: Once developed, strategies can be applied across multiple instruments simultaneously

### Challenges

1. **Technical Skills Required**: Need programming and statistical knowledge
2. **Market Changes**: Strategies that worked in the past may not work in the future
3. **Data Quality**: Requires access to reliable market data
4. **Infrastructure**: Needs stable computing systems and network connectivity
5. **Risk Management**: Automated systems can amplify losses if not properly monitored

## Key Concepts and Terminology

### Basic Terms

- **Strategy**: A set of rules that determine when to buy or sell
- **Signal**: An indication generated by your strategy to enter or exit a position
- **Backtest**: Testing a strategy using historical data to evaluate performance
- **Alpha**: The excess return of a strategy compared to a benchmark
- **Sharpe Ratio**: A measure of risk-adjusted returns (higher is better)
- **Drawdown**: The peak-to-trough decline in portfolio value
- **Slippage**: The difference between expected trade price and actual execution price

### Order Types

- **Market Order**: Buy/sell immediately at the best available price
- **Limit Order**: Buy/sell only at a specified price or better
- **Stop Order**: Triggers a market order when price reaches a certain level
- **Stop-Limit Order**: Combines stop and limit orders

### Position Management

- **Long Position**: Buying an asset expecting it to increase in value
- **Short Position**: Selling an asset (you don't own) expecting it to decrease in value
- **Position Sizing**: Determining how much capital to allocate to each trade
- **Portfolio**: Collection of all your trading positions

## Types of Quantitative Strategies

### 1. Trend Following (CTA Strategy)

- **Concept**: "The trend is your friend" - follow the direction of market momentum
- **Example**: Buy when price crosses above its 50-day moving average, sell when it crosses below
- **Time Frame**: Medium to long-term (days to months)
- **Markets**: Futures, commodities, forex

### 2. Mean Reversion

- **Concept**: Prices tend to return to their average value over time
- **Example**: Buy when price falls significantly below average, sell when it rises above
- **Time Frame**: Short to medium-term (hours to days)
- **Markets**: Stocks, indices, pairs trading

### 3. Statistical Arbitrage

- **Concept**: Exploit pricing inefficiencies between related instruments
- **Example**: Trade the price difference between two correlated stocks
- **Time Frame**: Very short-term (seconds to minutes)
- **Markets**: Stocks, ETFs, futures

### 4. Market Making

- **Concept**: Profit from bid-ask spreads by providing liquidity
- **Example**: Simultaneously place buy and sell orders at different prices
- **Time Frame**: Ultra-short-term (milliseconds to seconds)
- **Markets**: All liquid markets

### 5. Machine Learning / AI Strategies

- **Concept**: Use ML algorithms to predict future price movements
- **Example**: Train a neural network on historical data to forecast returns
- **Time Frame**: Variable (minutes to days)
- **Markets**: Stocks, futures, crypto

### Step-by-Step Breakdown

1. **Research & Idea Generation**
   - Study market patterns, read research papers, explore anomalies
   - Formulate hypotheses about potential trading opportunities

2. **Data Collection & Preparation**
   - Gather historical price data, volume, fundamentals
   - Clean data, handle missing values, adjust for corporate actions

3. **Strategy Development**
   - Code your trading logic in Python
   - Define entry/exit rules, position sizing, risk management

4. **Backtesting & Optimization**
   - Test strategy on historical data
   - Analyze performance metrics (returns, Sharpe ratio, drawdown)
   - Optimize parameters (but beware of overfitting!)

5. **Risk Assessment**
   - Calculate maximum drawdown, value at risk (VaR)
   - Set stop-loss levels and position limits
   - Ensure proper diversification

6. **Paper Trading**
   - Run strategy in real-time without real money
   - Verify that live performance matches backtest expectations
   - Test order execution and system stability

7. **Live Trading**
   - Start with small capital allocation
   - Gradually increase size as confidence grows
   - Monitor closely for unexpected behavior

8. **Monitoring & Refinement**
   - Track daily performance and risk metrics
   - Adjust strategy as market conditions change
   - Continuously improve and adapt

### Your First Strategy

Here's a simple moving average crossover strategy:

```python
from vnpy.app.cta_strategy import (
    CtaTemplate,
    StopOrder,
    TickData,
    BarData,
    TradeData,
    OrderData,
    BarGenerator,
    ArrayManager,
)

class MaCrossStrategy(CtaTemplate):
    """Moving Average Crossover Strategy"""
    
    # Strategy parameters
    fast_window = 10  # Fast MA period
    slow_window = 20  # Slow MA period
    
    # Strategy variables
    fast_ma = 0.0
    slow_ma = 0.0
    
    def __init__(self, cta_engine, strategy_name, vt_symbol, setting):
        super().__init__(cta_engine, strategy_name, vt_symbol, setting)
        
        self.bg = BarGenerator(self.on_bar)
        self.am = ArrayManager()
    
    def on_bar(self, bar: BarData):
        """Process new bar data"""
        self.am.update_bar(bar)
        if not self.am.inited:
            return
        
        # Calculate moving averages
        self.fast_ma = self.am.sma(self.fast_window)
        self.slow_ma = self.am.sma(self.slow_window)
        
        # Generate trading signals
        if self.fast_ma > self.slow_ma:
            # Golden cross - buy signal
            if self.pos == 0:
                self.buy(bar.close_price, 1)
        elif self.fast_ma < self.slow_ma:
            # Death cross - sell signal
            if self.pos > 0:
                self.sell(bar.close_price, 1)
        
        self.put_event()
```
